Protect Yourself from Email Phishing Attacks

Learn how to identify and avoid email phishing attempts that could compromise your personal information and online security.

Get Started

What is Phishing?

Email Deception

Phishing emails impersonate trusted entities like banks, social media platforms, or colleagues to trick you into revealing sensitive information.

Credential Theft

The goal is often to steal your usernames, passwords, credit card details, or other personal information that can be used for identity theft.

Protection Strategies

Learning to recognize phishing attempts is your first line of defense against these increasingly sophisticated cyber attacks.

Phishing Email Examples

Bank Security Alert Example

From: security-alert@nationaIbankservice.com

Subject: URGENT: Security Alert - Immediate Action Required

Date: March 11, 2025, 9:14 AM


Dear Valued Customer,


We have detected unusual activity on your account that requires your immediate attention. Our security system has identified several login attempts from an unrecognized device.


For your protection, we have temporarily limited access to your online banking services. To restore full account access, please verify your identity by following these steps:


1. Click on the secure link below to access our verification portal:
Secure Account Verification


2. Confirm your personal information and account details
3. Update your security preferences


This process will take approximately 2-3 minutes to complete. Failure to verify your account within 24 hours may result in a temporary suspension of your online banking access.


NOTE: This is an automated security measure and requires your prompt attention.


If you have any questions, please contact our customer service at 1-800-XXX-XXXX.


Thank you for your cooperation and for banking with us.


Sincerely,
Security Department
National Banking Services

Notice the misspelled domain: using "l" instead of "i" in "nationaIbankservice.com"
Creating urgency and fear is a common tactic to push victims into acting without thinking

How to Protect Yourself

1

Check the Sender's Email Address

Verify the sender's email domain. Banks and legitimate companies use their official domains, not public email services like gmail.com or hotmail.com. Watch for subtle misspellings like "paypaI.com" instead of "paypal.com".

2

Beware of Urgent Requests

Phishing emails often create a false sense of urgency to push you into making quick decisions. Be suspicious of emails claiming "immediate action required" or threatening negative consequences.

3

Hover Before You Click

Hover your cursor over links to see the actual URL before clicking. If the displayed link and the actual URL don't match, or if the URL looks suspicious, don't click it.

4

Never Share Sensitive Information

Legitimate organizations will never ask for passwords, full credit card numbers, or social security numbers via email. When in doubt, contact the organization directly using their official phone number or website.

5

Use Multi-Factor Authentication

Enable multi-factor authentication (MFA) on all your important accounts to provide an extra layer of security even if your password is compromised.

Test Your Knowledge

1. Which of the following is a common sign of a phishing email?

  • An urgent request requiring immediate action
  • An email from someone you regularly communicate with
  • A newsletter you've subscribed to
  • A receipt for a purchase you recently made
Correct! Phishing emails often create a false sense of urgency to pressure you into acting quickly without thinking.

2. What should you do if you receive a suspicious email claiming to be from your bank?

  • Click the link to see if it looks legitimate
  • Reply to the email asking for verification
  • Contact your bank directly using their official phone number
  • Forward the email to a friend to get their opinion
Correct! Always verify suspicious communications by contacting the organization directly through their official channels, not through the contact information provided in the suspicious email.
